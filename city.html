History

Tokyo began as a small fishing village called Edo. It gained in prominence as it was the residence of Tokugawa Ieyasu, who became shogun of Japan in 1603.


In 1869, the emperor Meiji moved to Edo, which became the capital of Japan. Subsequently, the name was changed to Tokyo, meaning “eastern capital”.


Though mostly destroyed during World War II, Tokyo has grown back to become a major world metropolis.

Attractions

Toyosu fish market

Opening in 2018, the Toyosu Market replaced the historic Tsukiji fish market as the largest seafood market in the world.


In the daily seafood auctions, the highest quality fish are a hotly contested commodity. The best tuna have sold for over 1 million yen per kilogram (about $10,000).


You can view the auctions from one of the observation decks. Be sure afterwards to stop by the restaurants and shops to enjoy some fresh seafood.

Ryogoku Sumo Hall

Opening in 1985, the Ryogoku Sumo Hall is home to three of Japan’s six yearly honbasho, i.e. major sumo tournaments.


A honbasho lasts for 15 days, beginning and ending on a Sunday. Results in these tournaments will determine each wrestler’s promotion or demotion in the official sumo rankings.

Utagawa Kunisada / Public domain

Shibuya

One of Tokyo’s major business and shopping centers, Shibuya is home to many department stores, as well as Shinjuku and Shibuya stations, the world’s two busiest train stations.


Shibuya is well known for the bustling scramble crossing in front of Shibuya Station’s Hachiko exit.


If you’re looking for something more peaceful, visit Yoyogi Park. Thousands of people visit Yoyogi Park each spring for cherry blossom viewing.
